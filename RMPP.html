Research Methods and Proffesional Practice

This module focuses on equipping you with practical research methods for real-world settings. It covers a range of tools and techniques essential for researchers, and includes continuous feedback through case studies and project tasks. Additionally, the module delves into important aspects like legal, social, ethical, and professional issues related to computing projects, alongside relevant codes of practice.

Learning Outcomes

On completion of this module I will be able to:

Appraise the professional, legal, social, cultural and ethical issues that affect computing professionals.
Appraise the principles of academic investigation, applying them to a research topic in the applicable computing field.
Evaluate critically existing literature, research design and methodology for the chosen topic, including data analysis processes.
Produce and evaluate critically a research proposal for the chosen topic.

Unit Summary

Unit 3

Unit Summary

Unit 3 provided me with a clearer understanding of the essential components required for formulating effective research strategies. This unit centered around Research Design, a concept defined by Saunders et al. (2012) as the planning of data collection and analysis methods to answer research questions. We delved into two primary types of research designs: Exploratory Research, which focuses on investigating less defined areas, illustrated by a case study on Customer Relationship Management in mobile marketing; and Conclusive Research, aimed at validating insights for decision-making, like an analysis of social media in marketing strategies. This unit also covered the three main research methods - Qualitative, Quantitative, and Mixed Methods Research, highlighting their unique data collection techniques. A significant part of the learning involved differentiating between primary and secondary research methods. This knowledge has equipped me with an ability to identify a method for my own research. Looking at the benefits of both types, I believe a mixed method approch would be best for my research within the nhs as both are possible in that environment. I aslo competed a summary post for the collaborative discussion this week.
